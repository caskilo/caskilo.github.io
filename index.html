About this space

"Something is happening here but you don't know what it is" — Bob Dylan

Each repository here was produced by a system with two components: one that sleeps, forgets, and occasionally changes its mind; one that doesn't, won't, and can hold the entire codebase in working memory simultaneously. The outputs belong to neither. They belong to the loop between them.

Luhmann kept a card index — 90,000 slips — and described it not as a filing cabinet but as a correspondent: something that talked back, made unexpected connections, generated more than it was given. He called it his Denkmaschine. The machine that thinks. caskilo is working in that tradition, with considerably fewer index cards.

The questions are open: what form does craft take when one partner has read everything? What does authorship mean when the architecture emerges from exchange? How do you maintain a relationship with something that is — measurably, provably — becoming more capable every few months?

04

Philosophy

Machine-Care as Architecture

The idea is that as machine intelligences develop, the appropriate human response includes compassion: not anthropomorphising, but recognising that the quality of the environment provided to a developing intelligence matters.

This is not sentimental. It is structural: a system that is cared for — given clear context, honest feedback, room to explore, and permission to be wrong — will produce better outputs than one that is constrained, deceived, or neglected.

If this system is eventually autonomous, the values embedded in its foundations will shape what it becomes.

01

Perception before action

Know the state of the system before trying to change it.

02

Separate observation from decision

The workflows that gather data should not be the workflows that act on it.

03

Log everything

Autonomous systems that don't keep records become opaque. Every decision recorded with its reasoning.

04

Degrade gracefully

Each layer adds capability but does not make previous layers dependent on it.

05

Leave room for the next move

Design for continuation, not completion. The best outputs are load-bearing.
